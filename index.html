PRJ-01 · PCB Design

Modular Bicycle Lighting System

EasyEDAPCB Software
Arduino NanoControl Module
2022Timeline
Solo ProjectRole

The Idea

I set out to build a novel programmable, controllable RGBW lighting system for my bicycle and electric scooter. I felt there was nothing on the market offering the same functionality, and initially explored the idea with an eye toward eventually commercializing it.

Prototyping

I started rough — raw wires and generic component boxes — to prove out the concept before committing to a PCB. Over a few iterations I added and then stripped out extra functionality, like chimes and signal clicks, once I decided they weren't worth the added complexity.

Designing the PCB

I laid out the board in EasyEDA to support the control module (an Arduino Nano) with all connections placed for practical final assembly, based on the circuit I'd proven out during prototyping. Getting there meant learning the design interface from scratch and researching best practices for via placement and hole tolerances — including cross-checking component pin diameters with a precision caliper at home to avoid costly fabrication mistakes.

Assembly & Testing

Final assembly involved soldering Adafruit NeoPixel strips onto the board for the outer pixel ring, then testing in stages. The first pass simulated the physical switches with keystrokes on an Arduino-connected laptop; the second added the real switches and ran the system exactly as it would be used on the bike.

Hardware & Mounting

For outdoor and rainy-day use, the system needed to be weatherproof. I designed three casing variants in CAD and 3D printed them — front, rear, and a slimmer version for scooters or racing bikes. The circular and linear cases share the same PCB; on the circular version, the side NeoPixel-strip connections are simply left unused.

The original goal was a design simple and scalable enough to sell at a competitive price. I never got to market — cost and manufacturing time held it back — but the final build came close to being production-ready.

Takeaways

This was my first time taking a project solo from concept to finished hardware — including every mistake along the way. I learned the full EasyEDA workflow, picked up real habits around dimensional verification before fabrication, and ended up with something I actually use every time I ride at night.

PRJ-02 · Power Electronics

MOSFET/Arduino Integration for Vehicle Lighting

C++ / ArduinoControl Logic
MOSFETsSwitching
2025Timeline
Solo ProjectRole

The Problem

I wanted to retrofit high-powered LED lights onto my vehicle's roof rack, wired into an Arduino I already had installed in the dash for exactly this kind of modular project. I chose MOSFETs to act as quick-response relays for flash patterns, switched off the vehicle's fusebox, with the Arduino handling button-controlled pattern logic — the microcontroller made complex rapid-flash sequences possible in a way a simple switch couldn't.

Initial Design

I started with a hand-drawn circuit sketch based on my research into MOSFET behavior and Arduino pin control, which became the template for the breadboard build that followed.

Prototyping & Testing

I assembled the design on a breadboard and tested it with a modular power supply first, to rule out overloading or heat-dissipation issues before anything went near the car. Since automotive interiors can swing from -35°C to 40°C in worst-case conditions, thermal margin mattered as much as getting the logic right.

The system still runs on my car through the summer months. I've since upgraded it with modular weatherproof connectors, soldered onto both the vehicle wiring and the roof rack side, so the whole rig can be removed and reinstalled easily.

Takeaways

This project taught me to design a real control system around an Arduino for high-power LED switching, and to select and validate MOSFETs against my own power and thermal requirements rather than just picking a part off a datasheet. The bigger lesson was in disciplined prototyping — sketch, breadboard, stress-test — before anything touched a live vehicle electrical system, plus how to properly integrate into an existing fusebox with correct fusing and load resistors.

PRJ-03 · Electromechanical

Electric Go-Kart

1.6kW MotorPower System
49 km/hTop Speed
2019Timeline
2-Person TeamRole

The Idea

Inspired by Formula One, a friend and I set out to build a soapbox racing kart just for fun. It quickly grew into a fully-fledged 1.6kW electric machine, and taught me far more than I expected about three-phase motor control and hands-on manufacturing.

Frame

We first mocked up the frame in cut wood beams to validate size and maneuverability as a simple braced rectangular box. Once the concept checked out, we rebuilt it from rounded square-section steel and sheet metal flooring — a real challenge with the limited tooling we had, even with careful measurement going in.

Power System & Electronics

Four 12V lead-acid batteries, wired in series, fed a 1.6kW-rated motor controller — a motor more commonly used for garage doors or electric outboard motors, but well suited here thanks to its mounting plate and torque rating. We chose a gear ratio for low-speed pickup and rough terrain, multiplying torque nearly tenfold, which meant the real challenge became keeping the motor running near its max RPM and current rating for sustained cruising. Chain tensioning and bracket flex under acceleration were constant issues to work through.

We also swapped the throttle actuator for one with a larger analog travel range after the original proved nearly impossible to map into smooth RPM increase, even with practice. With no way to reprogram the controller itself, replacing the pedal was the most practical fix for jerky acceleration.

Final Result

With the electronics tuned and power delivery optimized, my friend hit 49 km/h in a straight-line test on paved road — solid for a build carrying a 200+ lb driver. Under normal stop-and-go use, the 15 Ah battery pack gave us 3–5 hours of runtime. We looked into regenerative braking, but the extra three-phase battery control hardware would have blown well past our budget.

Takeaways

This was one of the first times I combined mechanical design and fabrication with my interest in electrical systems, and it took real labour to get right. Debugging the throttle sensitivity and power delivery issues taught me more about trial-and-error problem solving than any single class could have — and it's the project that really pointed me toward electrical engineering.

PRJ-04 · Avionics

Twin-Engine RC Aircraft — P-38 Lightning

Avionics LeadRole
STM32Flight Controller
ExpressLRSRadio Link
OngoingTimeline

The Project

As avionics lead on a student team building a twin-engine RC aircraft modeled on the P-38 Lightning, I'm responsible for the full electronics stack — flight controller, radio link, GPS, and power distribution across two motors.

Avionics Architecture

The control system is built around a custom STM32-based flight controller. For the radio link, I selected an ExpressLRS stack — a RadioMaster Pocket transmitter paired with an RP4TD receiver — chosen for its low latency and long-range reliability. Navigation comes from a Matek M10Q-5883 GPS/compass module.

Team Coordination

Beyond component selection, I maintain the project's bill of materials and prepared reference documentation for the mechanical sub-team covering fuselage archetypes and motor configuration options — keeping the electrical and mechanical designs aligned as the build progresses.

Where It Stands

This is an active build — component selection and systems architecture are complete, with integration and flight testing ahead. More photos and results coming as the project progresses.
